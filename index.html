Ten years of response times

The wait for an ambulance is getting longer.

This isn't modelling or estimate — it's the ACT Government's own data, reported to the Productivity Commission. Priority 1 response times held steady near 14 minutes until 2020-21, then climbed sharply to 17.8. The ACT may rank well against other states, but ranking fastest isn't the same as being safe — and the line is heading the wrong way. Closing this gap means more paramedics on the road.

Why it matters

Being the best in the country isn't the same as being safe.

The ACT often ranks among the quickest ambulance responses in Australia. That's a credit to the paramedics — not a reason for complacency. Ranking well isn't the same as being safe: every number that makes the service look strong is being held up by people working understaffed shifts, and even here the warning lights are flashing.

Demand keeps climbing

ACT ambulance responses rose from 49,734 in 2015-16 to 68,366 in 2024-25 — a 37% increase in a decade. Total incidents now top 55,000 a year, and the growth shows no sign of slowing.

Source: Report on Government Services 2026, Table 11A.4

The wait is getting longer

The Priority 1 response time held near 14 minutes for years, then climbed to 17.8 minutes by 2024-25 — up 30% over the decade. However the ACT ranks against other states, the trend is moving the wrong way, and more crews are needed to turn it around.

Source: Report on Government Services 2026, Table 11A.5

Stations are going dark

ACT ambulance stations were closed at least 68 times in the 18 months from April 2024 due to staffing shortages, and 42 times — around 260 hours — in the first quarter of 2026 alone. The Chief Officer's stated goal is zero closures; getting there means more crews.

Source: The Canberra Times & FOI disclosures, 2026; ACTAS quarterly report Q1 2026
